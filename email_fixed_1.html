Can We Tickle Ourselves Silly?

Imagine this: someone approaches you in a tickling mood, and you start giggling before they even touch you. Now try wiggling your own fingers and tickling yourself….nothing. It’s weird, isn’t it? Despite our best efforts, we simply can’t tickle ourselves. But why?


It all comes down to your brain's brilliant ability to predict the future—or at least your body's immediate future. When you perform an action yourself, your brain anticipates exactly how it's going to feel. Before your fingers even brush against your skin, your cerebellum (a region at the back of your brain) sends signals that effectively warn other parts of your brain: "Don’t get excited, this is just us."


This predictive system dulls the sensation, ensuring self-generated touches feel much less intense. It's your brain's way of staying alert to unexpected stimuli, helping you differentiate between what's safe (your own touch) and what might actually pose a threat (like a sneaky sibling’s surprise attack).


Interestingly, researchers have tested this by designing robotic tickling machines that introduce slight delays or alter the movements just enough that the brain can’t perfectly predict the sensation. Suddenly, even self-directed tickling becomes genuinely ticklish again!


This phenomenon highlights just how sophisticated and nuanced the human brain is. So, next time dinner conversations go quiet, try asking, "Why can’t we tickle ourselves?" It’s a fun, brain-teasing reminder of the complex ways our bodies and minds protect us, and, of course, an excellent excuse to see who at the table is ticklish🤭!
